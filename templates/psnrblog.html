PSNR MSE

In the field of image and video processing, two commonly used metrics for measuring the quality of an image or video are PSNR and MSE. In this blog post, we will discuss what PSNR and MSE are and how they are used in image and video processing.

Mean Squared Error (MSE)

MSE is a measure of the average squared difference between the original and the compressed image. It is computed by taking the average of the squared difference between each pixel of the original and compressed image.

MSE is expressed as:

MSE = 1/N * Σi=1 to N (Ii - Ki)²

where N is the total number of pixels in the image, Ii is the pixel value in the original image, and Ki is the pixel value in the compressed image.

The smaller the MSE value, the closer the compressed image is to the original image, which means that the image quality is better. However, MSE does not always correlate well with human perception of image quality, especially in cases where there are high frequency components or sharp edges in the image.

Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR)

PSNR is a measure of the ratio between the maximum possible power of a signal and the power of corrupting noise that affects the fidelity of its representation. It is a logarithmic measure of the ratio of the peak signal power to the mean squared error.

PSNR is expressed as:

PSNR = 10 * log10((MAX^2) / MSE)

where MAX is the maximum pixel value of the image (for 8-bit images, MAX = 255).

The higher the PSNR value, the better the quality of the compressed image, as it indicates a higher ratio of signal power to noise power. PSNR is widely used as a metric for evaluating the quality of compressed images or videos.

Comparison of PSNR and MSE

MSE and PSNR are two commonly used metrics for measuring the quality of an image or video. MSE provides a direct measure of the error between the original and compressed image, while PSNR provides a logarithmic measure of the signal-to-noise ratio.

PSNR is a more intuitive measure of image quality than MSE, as it correlates better with human perception of image quality. It is also more widely used in industry standards for video compression, such as MPEG and H.264.

However, both MSE and PSNR have limitations, as they do not always capture the perceptual quality of an image or video accurately. For example, they may not account for the presence of artifacts, such as blockiness or blurring, that are often introduced during compression.

Conclusion

In summary, PSNR and MSE are two commonly used metrics for measuring the quality of an image or video in image and video processing. While both metrics have their limitations, they provide a useful quantitative measure of the quality of compressed images or videos. PSNR is more widely used in industry standards for video compression and is a more intuitive measure of image quality than MSE.
